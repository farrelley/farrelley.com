API's in the Federal Government

Open Data, Transparency, Public Trust, and Efficiency

Just after the 2009 Presidential Inauguration, President Obama said,

My Administration is committed to creating an unprecedented level of openness in Government. We will work together to ensure the public trust and establish a system of transparency, public participation, and collaboration. Openness will strengthen our democracy and promote efficiency and effectiveness in Government. — Barack Obama

Since then a lot has happened in the Federal Government pertaining to open data specifically with Application Programming Interfaces or API’s. It all really started with data.gov. A site that was created to by the Executive branch to give access to high value datasets in a machine readable format. Most of these datasets where published large XML, CSV, and ZIP files. In fact this is still the case and you can find over 75,000 datasets available to you today. Note: Some XML file formats on data.gov are in fact a web service. These links will redirect to the agencies web service page. Example: http://catalog.data.gov/dataset/airport-status-web-service. But since the creation of data.gov in May 2009 a lot has happened. In fact most of this has happened in last year or so. More and more agencies are moving away from the bulk data publishing method and moving to a more advanced realtime format method of using API’s. This really has been ruled by President Obama’s Open Data Policy Directive issued on May 9, 2013. This directive says,

“going forward, newly generated government data shall be made freely available in open, machine-readable formats, while appropriately safeguarding privacy, confidentiality, and security.” — forbes.com

This is really good news for everyone. Researchers, developers, and the public will be able to use this data for whatever they wish. With this executive order many more datasets will be coming online in the years to come.

So with this said lets checkout some of the API’s that the government has available for use, and how to find new ones.

Let’s first start with discovering new API’s.

There are a couple of places to look when looking for Government API’s. Many of these standards are used though out the Government websites.

  • When you are on a Government website try appending /developer or /developers to the URL.
  • Additionally you can try a subdomain by removing the “www.” and trying “developer.” or “developers.
  • Lastly you can check the General Services Administration GitHub page. Here they have a bunch of Git repositories that you might be interested in. However, there is one that lists all know Government API’s. The repository is called slash-developer-pages. In this repository there are many file formats that list all government /developer pages. If you navigate to any of these pages you will see information on that specific agencies open data initiative.

API’s you may be interested in.

  • Federal Aviation Administration - Airport Information
  • Department of Homeland Security - TSA Checkpoint Wait Times
  • Federal Register - Daily Journal of the Federal Government
  • National Library of Medicine - Pillbox

As you can see there are a vast variety of datasets. These are just a couple of examples that you may find interesting. More are coming online every month and it’s exciting to this come together.
